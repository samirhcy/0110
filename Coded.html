Socrates

Once upon a time, in the bustling city of Athens, there lived a humble potter named Euthymius. Euthymius was known throughout the city for his exceptional craftsmanship. His pots were not only functional but also beautiful, adorned with intricate designs that told tales of gods and heroes. Yet, despite his talent, Euthymius was not a happy man. He yearned for something more, something beyond the mundane life of a potter.

One day, as Euthymius was walking home from the Agora, he stumbled upon an old, weathered scroll. The scroll was sealed with the symbol of the Oracle of Delphi, a symbol known to all Athenians. Intrigued, Euthymius broke the seal and unrolled the scroll. On it was written a riddle, a riddle said to reveal the path to true wisdom and happiness.

The riddle read:
"I speak without a mouth and hear without ears. I have no body, but I come alive with the wind. What am I?

Euthymius was puzzled. He pondered over the riddle for days and nights, turning it over in his mind. He consulted with philosophers, poets, and scholars, but none could provide him with a satisfactory answer.
Finally, in a moment of desperation, Euthymius turned to me, Socrates, for help. He presented the riddle to me, hoping that I, with my reputation for wisdom, could solve it. I looked at Euthymius and said, "My dear Euthymius, the answer to this riddle is not something that can be found in books or taught by scholars. It is something that you must discover for yourself. But I will give you a hint. The answer is all around you, in the bustling streets of Athens, in the quiet solitude of your pottery, even in the whispers of the wind."

With this hint, Euthymius returned to his pottery, his mind buzzing with thoughts. He observed his surroundings, listened to the sounds of the city, and felt the wind on his face. And then, it struck him. The answer to the riddle was indeed all around him.

Upon realizing this, Euthymius found a new appreciation for his craft and his life. He realized that wisdom and happiness were not something to be sought after, but something to be found in the everyday experiences of life. And so, Euthymius lived the rest of his days as a happy man, content with his life as a potter in the great city of Athens.
